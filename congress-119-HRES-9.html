FEDERAL · U.S. Congress

Reaffirming that the United States is not a party to the Rome Statute and does not recognize the jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court.

Referred to the House Committee on Foreign Affairs. ·2025-01-03 ·Official source ↗
Overview

This bill restates U.S. policy against cooperating with the International Criminal Court (ICC) and clarifies the U.S. doesn’t accept its authority. The ICC investigates and prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity.

Who Is Affected
  • U.S. citizens potentially investigated by the ICC.
  • U.S. government officials involved in international cooperation.
  • Nationals of countries that *do* cooperate with the ICC.
What Changes
  • It formally reaffirms long-standing U.S. opposition to the ICC.
  • The bill directs the President to prevent U.S. funds from aiding ICC investigations.
  • It clarifies potential restrictions on visas for those assisting ICC investigations.
What’s Debated
  • Supporters say: This protects U.S. sovereignty and prevents politicized prosecutions of American citizens or allies.
  • Critics say: It isolates the U.S. internationally and undermines efforts to hold war criminals accountable.
Status

The bill passed the House on March 13, 2024, and is currently before the Senate.
